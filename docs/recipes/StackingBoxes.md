# Recipe: Stacking Boxes

<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step9.webp" alt="Step 9 Visual" width="256"> <img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step8.webp" alt="Step 8 Visual" width="256"> <img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/thinstackingdemo.webp" alt="Thin Corner Stacking Cap Boxes Demonstration" width="256">


In this recipe, we'll make stacking boxes which are [`8 CU x 8 CU x 4 CU`](/docs/design/Units.md), suitable for
e.g: organization of miscellaneous project materials. The steps in this recipe can be followed to construct
stacking boxes in any other sizing by adjusting the dimensions of the components involved. 

## Ingredients (per box)
- 1 `8 CU x 8 CU` [Plate](/docs/components/Plates.md)\*
- 4 `4 CU x 8 CU` Plates\*
- 8 `2 CU` [Corners](/docs/components/Corners.md)\*
- 4 `4 CU` Corners\*
- 4 [Corner Caps](/docs/components/CornerCaps.md), two of which are mirrored (in your slicer).
- 2 `4 CU` Protruding [Stacking Lips](/docs/components/StackingLips.md)\*
- 2 `4 CU` Intruding Stacking Lips\*
- 2 `2 CU` Protruding Stacking Lips\*
- 2 `2 CU` Intruding Stacking Lips\*
- 4 [Thin or Thick Corner Stacking Caps](/docs/components/CornerStackingCaps.md)
- (If using thick corner stacking caps): 4 [Stacking Pegs](/docs/components/StackingPeg.md)
- (If using thick corner stacking caps): 4 [Clipped Plugs](/docs/components/Plugs.md)


\*: Any same-size composite of smaller components will also work.

## Step 1
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step1.webp" alt="Step 1 Visual" width="256">

Start by placing down an `8 CU x 8 CU` plate to serve as the base of the box.

## Step 2
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step2.webp" alt="Step 2 Visual" width="256">

Slide four `4 CU` corners onto the plate as shown. If you find the corners difficult to slide on,
you may want to change the [clearance](/docs/design/Clearances.md) level to something looser. However, the tighter
components may still be usable, but you may need assistance from a rubber mallet
and/or pliers.

## Step 3
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step3.webp" alt="Step 3 Visual" width="256">

Slide four `2 CU` corners onto the plate. Try to align the resulting `6 CU` composite
corners so that each of their ends are `1 CU` inset from the outer boundary of the base plate
to approximate their final position, but this isn't strictly necessary.

## Step 4
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step4.webp" alt="Step 4 Visual" width="256">

Slide four `4 CU x 8 CU` plates onto the corners added in the previous step. Try to ensure that
the directionality of the protrusions is counter-clockwise, unlike in the picture for this step,
which shows them clockwise. If you do have them oriented as shown in the picture, you will need
to mirror the corner stacking caps listed in the ingredients in your slicer prior
to printing.

## Step 5
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step5.webp" alt="Step 5 Visual" width="256">

Flip the box over and slide the four corner caps onto slides so that the slides on the corner
caps contact the corner slides attached to the base plate. You *will* encounter interference
between the chamfer on the corner caps' stub slides and the edges of adjacent plates, but don't panic --
just apply steady force (pushing against the floor helps for this) to snap them
into place. Make sure to inspect each corner cap to ensure that they are seated properly -- you
can tell by whether or not the corner cap's outer chamfers align with the chamfers of the corner slides.

## Step 6
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step6.webp" alt="Step 6 Visual" width="256">

Flip the box back over and slide four `2 CU` corners on top of the corner caps, leaving
`1 CU` gaps at the top. If you don't care about stacking, you could stop the recipe here
(but maybe add `1 CU` corners to close the aforementioned gaps) and have perfectly
functional boxes. Otherwise, continue.

## Step 7
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step7.webp" alt="Step 7 Visual" width="256">

Slide on the intruding and protruding stacking lips as shown, leaving `1 CU` gaps on each side
of each plate.

## Step 8 (Thin Corner Stacking Caps)
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step8.webp" alt="Step 8 Visual" width="256">

If you opt for thin corner stacking caps over thick ones, finish off the boxes by sliding
those on. If you had picked clockwise protrusions in step 4, these will need to be mirrored.

**Done**

<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/thinstackingdemo.webp" alt="Thin Corner Stacking Cap Boxes Demonstration" width="256">

The above picture shows the finished box on the bottom with a partially-constructed identical box stacked on top.
This configuration is perfectly servicable, but potentially prone to having the the top box accidentally bumped
out of alignment in high-traffic areas. Additionally, larger, heavier boxes with the same construction may have too much
accumulated displacement in their walls, which could compromise the stacking mechanism employed here.
For a more robust alternative, continue.

## Step 9 (Thick Corner Stacking Caps)
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step9.webp" alt="Step 9 Visual" width="256">

If you opt for thick corner stacking caps over thin ones, finish off the boxes by sliding those on.
If you had picked clockwise protrusions in step 4, these will need to be mirrored.

## Step 10
<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/step10.webp" alt="Step 10 Visual" width="256">

For each other box that you want to be able to stack on your box, snap together four stacking pegs
and four clipped plugs at the corners of the base plate for the box, with the stacking pegs facing
downwards.

**Done**

<img src="/docs/images/boxconstruction/thickstackingdemo.webp" alt="Thick Corner Stacking Cap Boxes Demonstration" width="256">

If you stack the boxes, you will notice that the stacking pegs slide into the sockets of the thick corner stacking caps,
preventing lateral movement without physically lifting the box on top.
